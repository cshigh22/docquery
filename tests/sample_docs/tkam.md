Set in the fictional, Depression-era town of Maycomb, Alabama, To Kill a Mockingbird follows the childhood of Scout Finch as she and her brother Jem navigate the complexities of race, class, and morality. Their father, Atticus Finch, a principled lawyer, is appointed to defend Tom Robinson, a Black man falsely accused of raping a white woman. Despite Atticus providing clear evidence of Tom's innocence, the deeply ingrained prejudices of the town lead to a tragic conviction. Intertwined with this legal drama is the children's fascination with their reclusive neighbor, Boo Radley, who eventually emerges as a heroic figure when he saves the children from a vengeful attack. Through these experiences, the novel explores the "mockingbird" metaphor—the idea that it is a sin to destroy innocence—and serves as a powerful critique of racial injustice and the inevitable loss of childhood naivety.
